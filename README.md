# Stroke_Risk_Analysis_Excel_Project

Key Insights:

Age is the strongest factor influencing stroke occurrence. The senior age group recorded the highest number of stroke cases, followed by the middle-aged group. This indicates a clear increase in risk with age.

A higher number of stroke cases were observed among non-smokers. However, further analysis suggests this is due to a larger population of non-smokers in the dataset rather than a direct causal relationship.

Low-income individuals showed a significantly higher prevalence of hypertension (~64%), indicating a strong relationship between income level and underlying health conditions.

Stroke risk and stroke occurrence were more prominent among individuals with sedentary or low physical activity levels, highlighting lifestyle as a key contributing factor.

Private sector employees, particularly in the lower income segment, showed higher instances of hypertension and elevated stroke risk.

Non-vegetarian dietary habits were associated with relatively higher stroke occurrences compared to vegetarian groups.

Chronic stress presented mixed patterns while individuals with stress showed higher susceptibility in certain segments, overall stroke occurrence did not consistently align with stress levels, indicating the need for deeper analysis.

Stroke occurrences were higher among individuals who reported never smoking and not consuming alcohol. However, this observation is influenced by the larger population size within these groups in the dataset, and therefore requires further evaluation using stroke rates (proportion of affected individuals) to draw meaningful conclusions about risk.

Conclusion:
Overall, age, income level, physical activity, work pressure and medical conditions like hypertension emerge as the most influential factors affecting stroke risk. Lifestyle and socio-economic conditions play a significant role in shaping health outcomes.
